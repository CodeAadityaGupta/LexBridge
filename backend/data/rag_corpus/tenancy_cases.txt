TENANCY DISPUTES — INDIA

Landlord-tenant disputes in India are governed primarily by state-specific Rent Control Acts. In most states, a landlord cannot evict a tenant without providing due notice and obtaining a court order. Summary eviction (locking out a tenant without notice) is illegal under both the Transfer of Property Act, 1882 and applicable Rent Control legislation.

Wrongful eviction: If a landlord locks out a tenant without a court order, the tenant can file a complaint under Section 441 of the Indian Penal Code (criminal trespass). The tenant may also approach the Rent Controller for restoration of possession, which is a faster remedy than civil court. Courts have consistently held that self-help eviction — changing locks, removing belongings, or cutting off electricity — is unlawful regardless of whether the tenant owes rent.

Security deposit disputes: Under most state rent laws, a landlord is required to return the security deposit within 30 days of vacating. If the landlord refuses, the tenant can approach the Rent Control Court or file a civil suit for recovery of money. The burden of proof for any deductions from the deposit lies on the landlord, who must produce receipts for actual repair costs.

Notice period: Most rent agreements require 1–2 months notice from either party. If the landlord violates this, it strengthens the tenant's claim for compensation. A registered rental agreement is stronger evidence than an unregistered one, but both are admissible. WhatsApp messages and email exchanges between landlord and tenant are treated as electronic records under the Information Technology Act and are admissible as evidence.

Rent increase: A landlord cannot increase rent arbitrarily. Under most state Rent Control Acts, rent can only be increased by a prescribed percentage (typically 4–10% per year) and only after proper notice. Any agreement to waive tenant rights under the Rent Control Act is void.

Documents typically required in tenancy disputes: original lease or rental agreement, rent receipts, bank transfer records of rent payments, any written communication with the landlord (WhatsApp messages are admissible), photographs of the property condition at move-in and move-out.

Electricity and water disconnection: If a landlord illegally disconnects utilities to force a tenant to vacate, this is considered an act of criminal intimidation under Section 503 IPC. The tenant can file an FIR and simultaneously seek an injunction from the civil court to restore utilities.

Sub-letting: A tenant cannot sub-let the premises without the landlord's written consent unless the original agreement permits it. Unauthorized sub-letting gives the landlord grounds for eviction through the Rent Control Court.

Eviction grounds recognised by law: Eviction can be sought if the tenant has not paid rent for more than 2–3 months (varies by state), has caused material damage to the property, has used the property for illegal purposes, or the landlord genuinely requires the property for personal use (bona fide requirement). The landlord must prove bona fide need — speculative or vague claims are rejected.

Process for eviction: The landlord must first issue a legal notice (typically 15–30 days), then file an eviction petition before the Rent Controller. The process can take 6 months to 2 years. During this time, the tenant is protected from physical eviction.
