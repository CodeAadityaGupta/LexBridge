FAMILY DISPUTES — INDIA

Family law in India is governed by personal laws that vary by religion: the Hindu Marriage Act 1955, the Special Marriage Act 1954, the Muslim Personal Law (Shariat) Application Act 1937, the Indian Divorce Act 1869 (for Christians), and the Parsi Marriage and Divorce Act 1936.

Divorce by mutual consent: Under Section 13-B of the Hindu Marriage Act, spouses can jointly file for divorce if they have lived separately for at least one year. After filing, there is a mandatory cooling-off period of 6 months (reduced to none in some High Court decisions), following which the divorce decree is granted. This is the fastest route to dissolution and avoids contested proceedings.

Contested divorce: A spouse can seek divorce on grounds of cruelty, adultery, desertion (for at least 2 years), conversion to another religion, mental disorder, communicable disease, or the other spouse being presumed dead (missing for 7+ years). Cruelty is the most common ground and includes mental cruelty such as persistent verbal abuse, financial control, and social isolation.

Maintenance and alimony: Under Section 125 CrPC (now BNSS Section 144), a spouse who is unable to maintain themselves can claim maintenance from the other. The Supreme Court has held that maintenance should reflect the standard of living enjoyed during the marriage. Maintenance can be claimed even before divorce is finalised. Under the Hindu Adoption and Maintenance Act 1956, a Hindu wife has the right to claim maintenance from her husband's property.

Domestic Violence: The Protection of Women from Domestic Violence Act 2005 provides civil remedies for women facing physical, emotional, sexual, or economic abuse. A Magistrate can issue a Protection Order, Residence Order, Monetary Relief Order, or Custody Order within 3 days of an application. Violation of a Protection Order is a criminal offence punishable with up to 1 year imprisonment.

Child custody: Courts decide custody based on the best interest of the child. Below age 5, custody is almost invariably given to the mother. Above that age, the child's preference (if old enough), the parent's living situation, and stability are considered. Joint custody is increasingly being granted by Indian courts. Interim custody can be obtained quickly through an urgent application to the Family Court.

Stridhan: Stridhan includes all gifts given to a woman before, during, and after marriage — jewellery, clothes, cash, property. A husband or in-laws who retain or sell Stridhan without the wife's consent are guilty of criminal breach of trust under Section 405 IPC. The wife can file an FIR and also recover Stridhan through a civil suit.

Dowry harassment: Under Section 498-A IPC (cruelty by husband or relatives), a wife who faces harassment for dowry can file a complaint with the police. This is a cognizable and non-bailable offence. The husband and in-laws can be arrested. Mediation is often recommended as a first step, and courts have discouraged automatic arrests following the Rajesh Sharma case guidelines.

Property rights: The Hindu Succession Act 1956 (amended 2005) gives daughters equal coparcenary rights in ancestral property. A married daughter has the same rights as a son regardless of when the marriage occurred. A wife has no automatic right in her husband's self-acquired property during his lifetime but inherits an equal share upon his death.

Documents needed in divorce/family matters: Marriage certificate, proof of residence, income proof (salary slips, ITR), bank statements, photographs of the marriage, any written evidence of cruelty or harassment, medical records if applicable.
